Apple Sues OpenAI, Alleging a Coordinated Campaign to Steal Its Hardware Secrets

A Northern California federal filing accuses OpenAI's chief hardware officer and a former Apple engineer of running a poaching-and-exfiltration scheme aimed at Apple's unreleased device plans — right as the two companies remain Apple Intelligence partners.

Apple filed suit against OpenAI on Friday, accusing the AI company of a coordinated effort to obtain confidential information about Apple's unannounced products. The complaint, filed in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of California, centers on two named defendants: Tang Tan, OpenAI's chief hardware officer and a 24-year Apple veteran who led iPhone and Apple Watch product design, and Chang Liu, a former Apple senior electrical engineer.

Apple alleges Tan used the company's internal project code names during OpenAI recruiting, asked candidates still employed at Apple to bring physical components to interviews for "show and tell," and coached departing staff on how to evade Apple's exit security checks. Liu is accused of retaining an Apple-issued laptop after leaving for OpenAI and using a network bug to download dozens of confidential hardware files.

OpenAI has called the allegations baseless, saying in a statement it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets." Apple is seeking a jury trial, the return of its materials, and a redesign of any OpenAI hardware found to rely on its technology — with the device business reportedly targeted for a 2026 launch.

Hyundai's Atlas Humanoid Takes the Field at the FIFA World Cup Halftime Show

Boston Dynamics' Atlas performed goal celebrations and delivered the match ball during a Round of 16 halftime break — the first live-match deployment of a humanoid robot at a World Cup.
1st Humanoid robot at a World Cup match

Hyundai Motor, the tournament's official robotics partner, brought Atlas onto the pitch at New York/New Jersey Stadium during the Brazil–Norway match. The robot ran through football-inspired celebrations before handing the ceremonial ball to the referee, powered by what Hyundai calls retargeting technology, reinforcement learning, and whole-body control.

Hyundai also deployed its dog-like Spot robots for venue security across the tournament, framing the halftime spot as proof its robotics division has moved past controlled demos into live, unscripted environments.

Ant Group's Robbyant Ships a Six-Model Full Stack for Embodied Intelligence

In a single release week, Robbyant open-sourced perception, action, world-modeling, and control models — a coordinated bid to make Ant Group's robotics stack the default "universal brain" for cross-embodiment robots.
6 Models shipped in one week

Robbyant, Ant Group's embodied-AI unit, released LingBot-Vision and LingBot-Depth 2.0 for spatial perception, LingBot-VLA 2.0 — a 6B vision-language-action model pre-trained on 60,000 hours of real-world robot data across 20 robot configurations from 17 manufacturers — and LingBot-World 2.0, a world model capable of an hour of stable, drift-free generation at 720p/60fps.

On Shanghai Jiao Tong University's GM-100 bimanual benchmark, LingBot-VLA 2.0 outperformed rivals including π0.5 and GR00T N1.7, and deploys in under 130 milliseconds on consumer-grade hardware. Commercial pilots are already running with retail and logistics partners in China.

1X Unveils a 25-Joint Tendon-Driven Hand That Can Feel What It Touches

The new hand for 1X's NEO humanoid uses low gear ratios for genuine force feedback, letting it pick grapes, screw in light bulbs, and sign — while the company scales to 10,000 units this year.

1X Technologies' new hand ships with 25 degrees of freedom and tactile sensors across the fingertips that detect pressure, contact location, and shear force in real time. Unlike typical robotic grippers with 100:1 to 200:1 gear ratios, 1X uses 5:1 to 15:1 ratios that make every joint backdrivable — the hand can sense how hard it's being pushed rather than only pushing.

The hand is IP68-rated and food-safe, built end-to-end in-house across motors, tendons, and polymers. CEO Bernt Børnich calls it "an API to the physical world" — pre-orders are open with U.S. deliveries planned for 2026 at $20,000.
